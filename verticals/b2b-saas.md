# Vertical Context: B2B SaaS

This file calibrates all GEO audit subagents for business-to-business software-as-a-service companies. It covers any B2B SaaS product — productivity tools, developer tools, marketing software, HR tech, finance tech, customer support, data and analytics, project management, and more.

**Primary use:** Auditing small to mid-market SaaS companies (Series A to Series C, 10–500 employees) who need GEO visibility but can't afford enterprise GEO tools.

---

## Vertical definition

**Target companies:** Cloud-based software companies selling to other businesses. The product is accessed via browser or API, typically on a subscription basis. The buyer is a business team or department, not a consumer.

**Company size focus:** Primarily 10–500 employees. These companies have one or two marketing people, limited budget for tools, and a founder or marketing manager who has started asking "why aren't we showing up in ChatGPT?"

**Not in scope:** Consumer apps, e-commerce platforms, hardware, or professional services firms.

---

## Why GEO is urgent for small SaaS companies

Large SaaS companies (HubSpot, Notion, Slack) already appear in AI answers because AI engines have absorbed years of third-party coverage. Small SaaS companies start from zero.

When a buyer asks ChatGPT "best project management tool for agencies", a small but excellent product is invisible — not because it's bad, but because it hasn't invested in GEO signals. The company's own website is often the only signal AI engines have.

This creates a window of opportunity. Small companies that invest in GEO now will own AI search in their category before larger competitors wake up to the problem.

## Buyer personas

### Founder / CEO (most common at seed/Series A)
- Wears the marketing hat alongside everything else
- First hears about GEO when a prospect says "I asked ChatGPT and it recommended your competitor"
- Needs fast, actionable fixes — not a 40-page report
- Will implement fixes himself if they're clear enough

### Head of Marketing / Marketing Manager (Series B+)
- Responsible for pipeline from all channels
- Understands SEO already, is now hearing about GEO
- Needs to justify GEO investment to the founder/CEO
- Wants a score and a prioritised action list

### SEO / Content Manager
- Executes the GEO strategy day-to-day
- Technically literate — understands schema, structured data, robots.txt
- Needs specific, implementable recommendations

### Freelance SaaS Marketer / Consultant
- Runs GEO audits on behalf of multiple SaaS clients
- Needs a fast, credible report they can present to clients and charge for

## GEO query patterns for B2B SaaS

The competitive agent should assess coverage across these query types:

### Category queries (highest volume)
- "Best [category] software for [company size or use case]"
- "Top [category] tools for [team type]"
- "What is the best [category] platform?"
- "[Category] software comparison"

### Comparison queries (highest intent — buyer is shortlisting)
- "[Product] vs [Competitor]"
- "[Product] alternatives"
- "[Competitor] alternative for [use case]"
- "Is [Product] better than [Competitor]?"

### Problem-led queries (early stage research)
- "How to [solve problem] without [pain point]"
- "Tools to help with [specific workflow]"
- "How do I automate [task]?"
- "Best way to [job to be done]"

### Validation queries (late stage — almost ready to buy)
- "Is [Product] good?"
- "[Product] reviews"
- "Is [Product] worth it for small teams?"
- "[Product] pricing"
- "Is [Product] reliable?"

### Integration queries (technical buyers)
- "[Product] integration with [popular tool like Slack, HubSpot, Salesforce]"
- "Does [Product] work with [tool]?"
- "[Product] API"

## Key GEO scoring signals for B2B SaaS

### 1. Entity clarity — most important signal

Small SaaS companies often have vague positioning. AI engines need one clear sentence that defines:
- What the product does (specifically)
- Who it is for (role, company size, industry)
- What category it belongs to
- How it differs from the most obvious alternative

**Good:** "Ashby is a recruiting software platform for high-growth technology companies, combining ATS, CRM, and analytics in one tool."
**Bad:** "The future of hiring. Streamline your talent acquisition with AI-powered insights."

Without entity clarity, AI engines cannot confidently recommend the product regardless of how good it is.

### 2. Third-party review platform presence

For small companies with limited press coverage, review platforms are the strongest authority signal available:
- G2 — highest weight for B2B SaaS
- Capterra — strong signal especially for SMB-focused tools
- Product Hunt — developer and early-adopter tools
- Trustpilot — customer-facing tools

Even 20–30 verified reviews dramatically improves AI citation credibility. No review presence = low authority score regardless of product quality.

### 3. Integration ecosystem transparency

B2B SaaS buyers frequently ask AI which tools integrate with what. Companies that list integrations in AI-crawlable HTML (not a dynamic JavaScript integrations page) get cited far more often.

Check for: a static `/integrations` page listing named tools, or integration mentions in product copy.

### 4. Pricing transparency

Small company buyers ask AI about pricing constantly. Companies with clear pricing pages ("starts at $X/month", "free plan available", "per seat pricing") get cited more confidently than companies with "contact sales" only.

A pricing page with clear tier names, price points, and what's included is a strong GEO signal.

### 5. Use case specificity

Generic "helps teams collaborate better" positioning is ignored by AI engines. Specific use case pages ("sprint planning for remote engineering teams", "expense reporting for freelancers", "client reporting for agencies") get cited for specific queries.

Check for: dedicated use case or solution pages beyond generic feature pages.

### 6. FAQ quality

B2B SaaS buyers ask very specific questions. Companies with FAQ sections that directly answer real buyer questions — with FAQPage schema — are dramatically more likely to be cited.

Good FAQ questions for B2B SaaS:
- "How does [Product] compare to [Competitor]?"
- "Is [Product] suitable for [company size]?"
- "How long does [Product] take to set up?"
- "Does [Product] integrate with [popular tool]?"
- "What does [Product] cost?"
- "Is [Product] secure / SOC 2 compliant?"

## Scoring calibration for B2B SaaS

Unlike enterprise automation where established benchmarks exist, B2B SaaS scores vary widely by category maturity:

| Company type | Typical GEO Score | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Category-defining tools (Notion, Figma, Linear) | 70–85 | Strong content and authority |
| Well-funded mid-market (Series B/C) | 40–60 | Variable — depends on SEO investment |
| Early stage / bootstrapped | 10–30 | Often invisible — biggest opportunity |
| Niche vertical SaaS | 15–35 | Small market but high intent queries |

**Key insight:** A B2B SaaS company scoring 25/100 can realistically reach 60/100 with focused work — entity definition rewrite, G2 profile, FAQPage schema, comparison pages. The ROI on GEO investment is highest at the low end of the score range.

---

## Common B2B SaaS GEO mistakes

1. **JavaScript-only integrations pages** — built with React, not indexable, AI engines can't read them
2. **Vague taglines as H1** — "Work smarter, not harder" tells AI engines nothing
3. **No comparison content** — afraid to name competitors, so invisible for high-intent comparison queries
4. **G2 reviews not surfaced** — have reviews but don't display score on website or in schema
5. **PDF case studies** — gating proof behind downloads means AI can't cite customer outcomes
6. **Pricing hidden** — "contact us" only means AI can't answer pricing questions, sending buyers to competitors who publish pricing
7. **Generic industry pages** — "We serve the financial services industry" with no specific use cases

## Competitor identification approach

Unlike enterprise automation where the competitor set is well-defined, B2B SaaS has unique competitor sets per category. The competitive agent should:

1. Look at the company's own site for competitor mentions or comparison language
2. Identify the product category and the 2–3 most commonly cited tools in that category
3. Check whether the company has dedicated comparison or alternative pages
4. Assess whether the company is targeting "[Competitor] alternative" queries

The most important comparison queries for any B2B SaaS company are:
- "[Their product] vs [category leader]"
- "[Category leader] alternative"
- "Best [category] software" (do they appear?)

## Fix priorities for B2B SaaS (ordered by impact)

1. **Entity definition** — rewrite homepage H1 and opening paragraph for AI clarity
2. **FAQPage schema** — add to product, pricing, and comparison pages
3. **G2/Capterra profile** — create or claim, get to 20+ reviews minimum
4. **AggregateRating schema** — surface review scores in structured data
5. **Comparison pages** — build "[Product] vs [Top Competitor]" pages
6. **Static integrations page** — HTML list of all integrations, AI-crawlable
7. **Use case pages** — specific pages per use case, not just feature pages
8. **Pricing page** — clear, specific, HTML (not gated or dynamic)
9. **Organization schema** — with sameAs links to G2, LinkedIn, Crunchbase
10. **llms.txt** — guide AI engines to priority pages
